Habitable Exoplanets

Introduction

I’ve always been fascinated by astronomy and the vastness of outer space. One thing I marvel at is the possibility of there being life on other planets. In this project, I explore these fascinating worlds with a focus on those that may possibly harbor life. The data was sourced from the NASA Exoplanet Archive. I used Microsoft SQL Server to clean and explore the data and Tableau to create visualizations that analyze their characteristics and distributions.

Analyzing the Data

Exoplanets are planets that lie outside our solar system, orbiting other stars. At the time of writing this, over 5,000 exoplanets have been discovered. Among them, habitable exoplanets are of particular interest to the scientific community, as finding conclusive signs of life would have immense impacts on our understanding of biology, how life arose here on Earth, and our place in the universe. It also provides targets for future space missions and research.

The field of exoplanet research is relatively new, with the bulk of discoveries made in recent years. The trend of exoplanet discovery over time is illustrated here, demonstrating the increasing pace of discovery thanks to advancements in technology and methods of detection.

Exoplanet Distance from Earth

The distance between stars is unbelievably vast. These scatter plots show the exoplanet’s relative distance from Earth measured in parsecs. 1 parsec equals 3.26156 light years, or roughly 19 trillion miles! Even the closest planets are mind-blowingly far from us.

Distribution of Exoplanets by Mass

Exoplanets are categorized into many different classes based on their mass. I chose the following classifications using Earth’s mass as the unit of measurement.

Terrestrial – 0.01 to 1 Earth masses
Super Earth – 1 to 11 Earth masses
Mini-Neptune – 11 to 17 Earth masses
Neptunian – 17 to 300 Earth masses
Gas Giant – 300 Earth masses and greater

Most discovered exoplanets are gas giants and Neptunian planets, as they are the easiest to detect. These types of planets are made mostly of gas and do not have solid surfaces, making the possibility of life highly unlikely. In contrast, terrestrial and super-Earth classes, which are rocky worlds, are the most likely to harbor life but are much rarer.

Distribution of Exoplanets by Star Type

Stars, like planets, fall into distinct categories based on mass and luminosity. Here is a graph showing the various types of stars in the universe.

Credit:The classification of Stars (Atlas of the Universe).

The following graphs show the various types of stars in the universe and break down the number of exoplanets found based on the types of stars they orbit. For all exoplanets, including habitable ones, the most common host stars are G-type stars, the same category as our Sun.

From left to right, the types of stars on the graph are larger, brighter, and have shorter lifespans. Planets orbiting cooler, longer-lived stars (especially sun-like stars) have a higher chance of harboring life. M-type stars, or red dwarfs, can live for trillions of years. Given the extensive time a planet in this type of star’s habitable zone has to develop life, rocky planets orbiting these stars are more likely to develop life over the span of their existence.

Conclusion

This project highlights the potential for discovering habitable planets outside of our solar system. The insights gained can guide our search for extraterrestrial life. Finding life on other planets is extremely important for humanity as it can redefine what we know about biology, the origins of life on our planet, and our place in the universe. It is a scientific endeavor that tackles fundamental questions about our existence and our future in the cosmos, making it one of the most exciting pursuits in modern science.
